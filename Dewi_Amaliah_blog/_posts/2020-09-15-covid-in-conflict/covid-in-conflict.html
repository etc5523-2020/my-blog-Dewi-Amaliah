COVID and Conflict: Why the pandemic could be worse in Afghanistan?

This article aims to convey the reason why the conflicted country, in this case is Afghanistan, might be hit harder by COVID-19.

Introduction

Since being declared as a global pandemic by the World Health Organization (WHO) on 12 March 2020, COVID-19 has affected 213 countries, including the conflict-torn country, Afghanistan (worldometer 2020). This country has its first confirmed case recorded on 24 February 2020 in Herat Povince after a citizen who had just returned from Iran contracted with the virus (Wikipedia 2020a).

Viruses are indeed indiscriminate. Unfortunately, the presence of COVID-19 amid this war-shattered country seems to rub salt into the wound. According to the United Nations Office for the Coordination of Humanitarian Affairs (OCHA) data, as of 6 September 2020, there were already 151,190 people displaced by the conflict (United Nations Office for the Coordination of Humanitarian Affairs 2020). Further, in 2019, civilian casualties due to the conflict has surpassed 100,000 (United Nations Assistance Mission in Afghanistan 2020). The Secretary-General’s Special Representative for Afghanistan and head of the UN Assistance Mission in Afghanistan (UNAMA) said as follows:

“Almost no civilian in Afghanistan has escaped being personally affected in some way by the ongoing violence” - Tadamichi Yamamoto (United Nations Assistance Mission in Afghanistan 2020)

In other words, the Afghan people are already vulnerable before COVID-19 attacks.

This article aims to convey the reasons why the conflict brings the pandemic getting worse in Afghanistan. The analysis would be focused on two aspects, namely health care and economic vulnerability.

Data Source

In doing analysis, I used several data sets from various sources. For the COVID-19 situation in Afghanistan, I used the data of confirmed cases and deaths from John Hopkins University Center for System Science and Engineering (JHU CSSE) (John Hopkins University CSSE 2020) and the population data from the World Bank (World Bank 2020). I also used the mobility data from Google COVID-19 Community Mobile Report (Google 2020) to analyze the population vulnerability. These data have been compiled in the tidycovid19 R package by Joachim Gassen (Gassen 2020), hence I directly downloaded it from this package. For the health care vulnerability analysis, I mainly used the data of health sector profile from the Afghanistan National Statistics and Information Authority (National Statistics & Information Authority of Afghanistan 2020) and the government national budget data from the Afghanistan Ministry of Finance (Islamic Republic of Afghanistan Ministry of Finance 2019).


Analysis Tools

The analysis is performed using R (R Core Team 2020). The data is wrangled mainly using the tidyverse function (Wickham et al. 2019). Further, the data is visualized using the function in ggplot2 (Wickham 2016), gghighlight (Yutani 2020) and ggrepel (Slowikowski 2020). I also used gt (Iannone, Cheng, and Schloerke 2020) and and scales (Wickham and Seidel 2020).

COVID-19 Cases in Afghanistan

To analyze the COVID-19 situation in Afghanistan, I used the cumulative numbers of confirmed cases and deaths. It is because I only want to see the general condition of COVID-19 in this country.

Figure 1: COVID-19 confirmed cases and deaths in Afghanistan 24 February to 15 September 2020

Figure 1 shows that until 15 September 2020, the number of COVID-19 cases in Afghanistan has reached 38,815, or equivalent to 1020.33 cases per one million population. We can also see that the curve flattened.

Regarding the death case, as of 15 September 2020, the number is 1,426 with 3.67 percent of case fatality rate. However, we could not conclude anything from this indicator because the WHO said that the fatality rate varies in each country from 0.1% to more than 25%. The calculation method is also still rough (World Health Organization 2019).

If we only look at the confirmed case number, we might conclude that Afghanistan has handled this pandemic well, given the evidence that the curve started to flatten. However, how reliable is the data? Of course, we cannot see this indicator alone. We also have to take other indicators into account, namely the testing rate and the positive rate. Unfortunately, it is tough to find Afghanistan’s data for these indicators. Our World In Data, the source of testing data in tidycovid19 package also does not have this indicator data for Afghanistan.

Further, according to the WHO, the actual rate of transmission is mostly underestimated since the disease is sometimes asymptomatic (World Health Organization 2019). The low testing capacity in the country then exacerbated the situation. According to Nicholas Bishop, an expert of the United Nations International Organization for Migration (IOM), as per June 2020, Afghanistan only held 646 tests per million population, bringing it to be one of the countries with lowest testing in the world (Changoiwala 2020). He also added that the true cases of COVID-19 in Afghanistan could be millions since there is a significant level of community transmissions across 34 provinces. Similarly, a survey conducted by KabulNow concluded that the actual cases in Kabul could be ten times more than the official record (Haidar 2020).

Why it could be worse?

Assuming that the actual cases are much higher than the official record, we could see an already bad situation in this war-torn country. Unfortunately, as I mentioned in the introduction, Afghan people are already miserable enough because of the conflict. Hence in this part, I would emphasize the conflict effects that made the pandemic worse in this country.

Health Care Vulnerability

The prolonged conflict in the past 18 years in Afghanistan has shifted the government focus to defense and safety affairs. Figure 2 suggests that more than 40 percent of the government’s budget plan in 2020 is for defense and public order and safety. The fund for public health only accounts for 4.4 percent, much lower than the other budget.

Further the health care facility in Afghanistan could be considered as inappropriate. In the three years of 2017 to 2019, there is no significant progress in health facilities provision in Afghanistan (refer to Table 1). The number of health associate professional even decreased in 2019. Further, the latest available data show that there are only three doctors for every 10,000 Afghans. Meanwhile, there are only five hospital beds per 10,000 population.

This reality is even more bitter because health facilities and their workers are often the targets of non-state armed groups. According to the UN Assistance Mission in Afghanistan report, 12 deliberate attacks on health care have happened during the pandemic (Devi 2020). One of them was carried out by armed men who attacked Dasht-e-Barchi hospital in Kabul on 12 May 2020. This attack killed 24 people, including 16 women and two babies. After this incident, Médecins Sans Frontières (MSF), a medical humanitarian organization, stopped its activities at this hospital, which means that one health facility was depleted, even though the need is increasing due to the pandemic.


Economic Vulnerability

Conflict does affect not only health care but also the economy. Afghanistan is one of the world’s poorest countries, and the perpetuated civil war is the culprit (Wikipedia 2020b) and (Xinhua 2018). Even before COVID-19 strikes, Afghans lived with an average income of less than 2 US dollars a day (Devi 2020). With this poverty level, the government will find it challenging to continue to apply the restriction that has been going on since early March (Cancho and Pradhan 2020). If this happened, there would be a new threat, a hunger (Glinski 2020).

Figure 3: Community mobility index in Afghanistan amid COVID-19

We can infer from Figure 3 that when the restriction was implemented, there was a decrease in community mobility almost in every place. However, in early July, the mobility to grocery and pharmacy, as well as retails and recreation, has increased again. It is because most of the population works as shopkeepers, street or retail sales, or daily labor in construction, agriculture, or personal services.

The poverty as the result of conflict made Afghans have no choice but to survive. That being said, returning to work amid a pandemic will undoubtedly increase the risk of being infected with COVID-19.
